# Boy

The medieval alleyway was narrow and dark, the cobblestones slick with recent rain. Twelve-year-old Scoat huddled against the cold stone wall, his brown hair falling into his blue eyes as he tried to make himself smaller.

"Well, well, look what we have here," a harsh voice cut through the damp air.

Scoat's heart sank. Three older boys blocked the alley's entrance, their shadows stretching long in the fading afternoon light. The leader, a burly teenager with a cruel sneer, cracked his knuckles.

"The little orphan thinks he can hide from us," the leader taunted, stepping forward. "You owe us protection money, runt."

"I don't have anything," Scoat whispered, his voice trembling.

The leader grabbed Scoat by the collar, lifting him effortlessly. "Then you'll pay with your hide instead."

The first punch sent pain exploding across Scoat's jaw. He stumbled back against the wall, tasting blood. Another blow caught him in the stomach, stealing his breath. The third boy joined in, kicking his legs out from under him.

Curled in a ball on the cold ground, Scoat felt despair wash over him. No one would help him. No one ever did. He was just another abandoned child in a world that had already forgotten him existed.

As the kicks and punches continued, something strange began to happen. A warmth spread through his chest, unlike anything he'd ever felt before. It wasn't the warmth of blood or pain—it was something else, something powerful.

"Please stop," he begged, but his voice was lost in their laughter.

The warmth intensified, flowing down his arms into his hands. He looked down in amazement as a soft blue light began to glow between his palms. The light coalesced, forming into a perfect sphere about the size of an apple.

"What the—?" one of the boys stepped back, eyes wide.

The sphere pulsed with energy, casting an ethereal blue glow across the alley. Scoat could feel its power thrumming through him, responding to his desperate need for protection.

"Magic!" the leader hissed, but his fear was quickly replaced by greed. "Think of what we could do with—"

He never finished his sentence.

Acting on pure instinct, Scoat thrust his hand forward. The blue sphere shot through the air, striking the leader square in the chest. The boy flew backward as if hit by a battering ram, crashing into the wall with a sickening thud and sliding to the ground unconscious.

The remaining two bullies stared in terror, then turned to flee.

"No," Scoat whispered, and another sphere appeared in his other hand. This one was smaller, faster. He sent it flying toward the first boy, then another toward the second. Both went down without a sound.

Silence descended upon the alley. Scoat stood slowly, his body aching but his mind clear. He looked at his hands—his ordinary, dirty hands—that had just produced something extraordinary.

The blue spheres faded, but the warmth remained, a constant presence now just beneath his skin. He was no longer just Scoat, the abandoned orphan boy. He was something more.

Something powerful.

As he walked out of the alley, leaving the unconscious bullies behind, Scoat felt the weight of his old life falling away. The world hadn't changed, but he had. And for the first time in twelve years, he wasn't afraid.

He was ready.
